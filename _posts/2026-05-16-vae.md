It has been almost a decade since the publication of the original paper of Variational Autoencoder (VAE) [1], but today I still want to go over this paper as it has been the foundations for many cool algorithms coming after, e.g., stable diffusion. Personally, the first time I get to knwo VAE is at my second year master course "Advanced Probabilistic Machine Learning", at that time, I become quit interested in this sort of probabilitic generative modeling that can generate unseen images.

Where does the "Variational" comes from?
======
Previously, Autoencoder is a model that sort compress the information down to a latent space, then recover that information from the latent space. However, the latent space does not follow a particular probability distribution and therefore there is no contraint on how this latent space looks like. The loss function for autoencoder can simply be computing the mean square error between the reconstructed image and the original image. The "Variational" comes from the fact that instead of just some free-form latent space, we will constrain this latent space to have the prior distribution we specify for it, which is usually Gaussian distribution $$N(0, 1)$$, so that later we can generate new images via sampling from this prior distribution.

What is the training objective?
======
If you are familar with deep learning, you are probably familar with the loss functions such as L1 loss, L2 loss and cross entropy loss. However, here the training objective of VAE is a bit sophisticated because we are actually training to fit a probability distributon rather than to fit the data points. For example, in L2 loss, we compare the prediction from the neural network with our groundtruth of the input data, which is simply $$|| \hat{y} - y ||^2 $$, however, in VAE, we aim to learn a distribution of our data, so that we can generate a new image by drawing a new sample from this learned distribution.

Following autoencoders, we can assume that our data can be assumed to be generated from a hidden space, and here we further assume that the hidden space can be defined by a probability distribution which we do not know now.

Formally, we can define the joint probability distribution as $$ p_{\theta}(x, z) = p(z)p_{\theta}(x|z) $$ parameterized by $$\theta$$, where $$z$$ is the unknown latent variables and $$x$$ is the data points (e.g., images of handwritten digits). Our goal is to learn the parameters $$\theta$$ that maximize the data probability, i.e., $$\arg \max_{\theta} p_{\theta}(x)$$, where $$p_{\theta}(x)$$ is the shorthand notation for $$p(x|\theta)$$.

Derivation of the loss function
------
Let's start by 
$$
\log p_{\theta}(x)
$$

, here we first apply Jensen's inequality and then introduce $$q_{\phi}(z \mid x)$$ by the trick $$ q_{\phi}(z \mid x) / q_{\phi}(z \mid x) = 1$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\log p_{\theta}(x) 
&= \log \int p_{\theta} (x, z) \frac{q_{\phi}(z|x)}{q_{\phi}(z|x)} dz \\
&= \log \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(z|x)}[\frac{p_{\theta} (x, z)}{q_{\phi}(z|x)}] \\
&\geq \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(z|x)}[\log \frac{p_{\theta} (x, z)}{q_{\phi}(z|x)}] 
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $$p_{\theta}(x|z) $$ is a distribution of $$x$$, the integral with repect to z for this distribution is just $$p_{\theta}(x|z) $$ itself. And the second term can be rewritten as the KL divergence between $$q_{\phi}(z|x)$$ and $$p(z)$$.
In the end, we have the following,

$$
\log p_{\theta}(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(z|x)}[\log \frac{p_{\theta} (x, z)}{q_{\phi}(z|x)}] 
$$

We want $$\max p_{\theta}(x)$$, which is equivalent to 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\max \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(z|x)}[\log \frac{p_{\theta} (x, z)}{q_{\phi}(z|x)}] = \max \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(z|x)}[p_\theta(x|z)] - \text{KL}(q_\phi(z|x)\| p(z))
\end{aligned}
$$
. 

We can inteprete this optimization problem as given $$z$$, we want to maximize the probablity of generating our data $$x$$ but minimize the KL divergence between $$q_\phi(z\mid x)$$ and $$ p(z) $$, thus it can seen as a regularizer term that forces $$q_\phi(z\mid x)$$ to be not that far away from $$p(z)$$. And $$ p_{\theta} (x \mid z)$$  is often called decoder as it sorts of decoding the data from the latent space, and $$q_\phi(z \mid x)$$ is often called encoder as it kinds of encoding the data into the latent space.

There is also another way to derive the loss function, which uses $$\log p_\theta(x) = \int (\log p_\theta(x)) q_\phi(z\mid x) dz $$. The full derivation is provided as below:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\log p_\theta(x)
&= \int (\log p_\theta(x)) q_\phi(z|x) dz \\
&= \int (\log  \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{p_\theta(z|x)}) q_\phi(z|x) dz \\
&= \int (\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{p_\theta(z|x)} \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{q_\phi(z|x)})  q_\phi(z|x) dz \\
&= \int (\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}) q_\phi(z|x) dz + \int q_\phi(z|x) \log \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{p_\theta(z|x)} dz  \\
&= \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}]}_{\mathcal{L}_{\theta, \phi}(x)} + \underbrace{\text{KL}( q_\phi(z|x) || p_\theta(z|x) )}_{\geq 0}
\end{aligned} 
$$

, where $$\mathcal{L}_{\theta, \phi} (x)$$ is called Evidence Lower Band (ELBO).

As a result, $$ \max \ell_{\theta, \phi}(x) \Leftrightarrow \max p_\theta(x) \Leftrightarrow \min \text{KL}( q_\phi(z\mid x) \| p_\theta(z \mid x) )$$

Reparamaterization Trick
======
If we want to further optimize our loss function via e.g. stochastic gradient descent (SGD), we will notice that while it is easy to compute the gradient with respect to $$\theta$$, it would be a bit tricky to compute the gradient with respect to $$\phi$$.

The gradient w.r.t $$\theta$$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\theta, \phi}(x) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x, z)] \approx \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \nabla_\theta p_\theta(x, z^{l}) \\
\text{where} \space z^{l} \sim q_\phi(z|x)
\end{aligned}
$$

The gradient w.r.t $$\phi$$:

$$
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}_{\theta, \phi}(x) = \nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[-\log q_\phi(z|x)]
\end{equation}
$$

Here, the problem comes from the fact that we want to compute the gradient w.r.t $$\phi$$ but the expectation is also w.r.t $$\phi$$. We can still do the usual Monte Carlo gradient estimator, which is 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[-\log q_\phi(z|x)] &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[-\nabla_\phi (\log q_\phi(z|x))\log q_\phi(z|x)] \\
&\approx - \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \log q_\phi(z^{l}|x) \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z^{l}|x)
\end{aligned}
$$

with $$z^{l} \sim q_\phi(z^{l}\mid x) $$. As we can notice, we actually sample from $$q_\phi(z \mid x)$$ then take the gradient on $$q_\phi(z \mid x)$$. Therefore this naive solution can have very high variance. 

The solution here is reparameterization trick, e.g., if we want to sample from $$N(\mu, \sigma^2)$$, it is equivalent to sample $$\epsilon$$ from $$N(0, 1)$$, then let $$ z = \sigma\epsilon + \mu$$. 

Let's reparameterize $$\tilde{z} \sim q_\phi(z \mid x)$$ with $$g_\phi(\epsilon, x)$$:

$$
\tilde{z} = g_\phi(\epsilon, x) \space \text{with} \space \epsilon \sim p(\epsilon)
$$
Then we can take the expectation w.r.t $$\epsilon$$ instead of $$z$$, i.e.,

$$
\begin{align}
\mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[-\log q_\phi(g_\phi(\epsilon, x))] 
\end{align}
$$

. As a result, we can compute the gradient w.r.t $$\phi$$,

$$
\begin{align}
\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[-\log q_\phi(g_\phi(\epsilon, x))] &= \mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[-\nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(g_\phi(\epsilon, x))] \\
&\approx - \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(g_\phi(\epsilon, x)) \\
&\text{where} \space \epsilon \sim p(\epsilon)
\end{align}
$$
